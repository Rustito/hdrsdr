How it works & what to expect

An HDR screenshot stores far more brightness than a normal screen can show. Crystal Rusty Clear reads those real values and folds them down to a standard image — and gives you a grading panel to make the SDR result look as close to the HDR original as possible.

What it can open

.hdr (Radiance) and .exr (OpenEXR, uncompressed) carry genuine float light values, so these are the formats where there's real HDR to recover. PNG, JPG, WEBP and AVIF open too, but browsers hand them over already clamped to SDR range, so the tools act as a normal color grade rather than true HDR recovery. Windows .jxr Game Bar captures can't be decoded by any browser — convert them to .hdr or .exr first (most HDR-aware editors and ffmpeg can do this).

What to expect on screen

After loading, the Peak white readout tells you the brightest value found. Above 1.00× means true HDR was read — a sunlit window might read 8× or more. The preview is a slider: the left side (CLAMPED) is the naive hard-clamp you'd get from just saving the file, and the right (GRADED) is your tuned result. Drag the handle to compare. The small curve graph shows how input scene light (left) maps to display brightness (right) with your current settings.

Step by step

  1. Load your .hdr or .exr. Check the peak — if it's well above 1×, there's HDR detail to bring back.
  2. Pick a curve. Start with ACES. If shadows look crushed, try Hable; for a softer photographic look try Reinhard.
  3. Set the White point to roughly your peak value (or a bit below). This decides which scene brightness becomes pure white and is the single biggest lever for taming blown-out highlights.
  4. Adjust Exposure so midtones sit where the HDR looked — brighter or darker overall.
  5. Fine-tune Highlights, Shadows, Contrast and Black point until the balance matches.
  6. Match the color with Saturation and Color rolloff, and fix the gamut if colors look off.
  7. Save PNG. Drag the compare slider first to confirm the recovery against the clamped version.

The controls

Curve
The shape that compresses bright scene light into the display range. ACES is neutral and film-like; Hable protects shadow detail; Reinhard is gentle; Clip just hard-cuts anything above white.
Exposure
Overall brightness in stops (EV). Each +1 doubles the light. Set this for the midtones.
White point
The scene value that becomes pure white. Raising it gives highlights more room before they blow out — the key control for recovering bright HDR areas.
Contrast
Steepens or flattens the curve around the midtones. Higher is punchier, lower is flatter.
Highlights
Brightens or recovers the upper range only. Pull it down to bring back detail in bright skies, lights and reflections.
Shadows
Lifts or deepens the dark range only. Lift to open up crushed shadows, lower to deepen them.
Black point
Sets where true black begins. A small amount adds depth; too much crushes shadow detail.
Saturation
Overall color intensity. HDR-to-SDR usually loses a little saturation, so a small boost often helps the match.
Color rolloff
Per-channel is the standard filmic look where bright colors fade toward white. Preserve hue keeps saturated highlights colorful — usually closer to how the HDR looked.
Source gamut
HDR files are often in a wider color space than SDR. If colors look over-saturated or wrong, switch to Rec.2020 or Display-P3 to convert them correctly into sRGB.

Getting the SDR closest to the HDR

  1. Curve = ACES, Color rolloff = Preserve hue.
  2. Set White point near the peak reading so the brightest spots just reach white without flattening.
  3. Nudge Exposure until the midtones match the HDR feel.
  4. Pull Highlights down slightly to recover detail in the brightest areas.
  5. Add a touch of Saturation (+5–15%) and a little Contrast to make up for the compression.
  6. If wide-gamut, set Source gamut to Rec.2020 so colors land correctly.
